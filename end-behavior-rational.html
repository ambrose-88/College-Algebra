    Section 6.5 End Behavior of Rational Functions

    Subsection 6.5.1 Other Types of Asymptotes

    Definition 6.5.1. Horizontal Asymptote.

    The horizontal line \(y=b\) is a horizontal asymptote for \(f(x)\) if \(y \to b\) as \(x \to \pm\infty\text{.}\)

    Note 6.5.2. Note.

    Graphs of functions may cross a horizontal asymptote.

    Definition 6.5.4. Oblique (Slant) Asymptote.

    The line \(y=mx+b\) is an oblique asymptote for \(f(x)\) if \(y\) approaches the graph of \(y=mx+b\) as \(x \to \pm\infty\text{.}\)

    Definition 6.5.6. Nonlinear Asymptote.

    A nonlinear function \(g(x)\) is a nonlinear asymptote for \(f(x)\) if \(y\) approaches the graph of \(g(x)\) as \(x \to \pm\infty\text{.}\)

    Subsection 6.5.2 End Behavior of Rational Functions

    Theorem 6.5.8. End Behavior Criterion.

    Let \(r(x)=\dfrac{p(x)}{q(x)}\) be a rational function. Let \(n\) be the degree of \(p(x)\) with leading coefficient \(a_n\text{,}\) and let \(m\) be the degree of \(q(x)\) with leading coefficient \(b_m\text{.}\) The end behavior is determined by comparing \(n\) and \(m\) as summarized below.

    Example 6.5.10. End Behavior.

    Find the end behavior of \(r(x)=\dfrac{4x^3-9x^2+4x-6}{5x^{8}-17x^7+3x-4}\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    \(r(x) \to 0\) as \(x \to \pm\infty\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Since the degree of the numerator is 3 and the degree of the denominator is 8, we see that the degree of the denominator is larger than the degree of the numerator. This shows the existence of a horizontal asymptote \(y=0\text{.}\)
    Therefore, \(r(x) \to 0\) as \(x \to \pm\infty\text{.}\)

    Example 6.5.12. End Behavior.

    Find the end behavior of \(r(x)=\dfrac{9x^{12}+3x^5-2x^2}{12x^{12}-4x^9+3x^7+x}\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    \(r(x) \to \dfrac{3}{4}\) as \(x \to \pm\infty\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Since the degree of the numerator is 12 and the degree of the denominator is 12, we see that the degrees of the numerator and denominator are equal. Therefore, there is a horizontal asymptote \(y=\dfrac{a_n}{b_m}=\dfrac{9}{12}=\dfrac{3}{4}\text{.}\)
    Therefore, \(r(x) \to \dfrac{3}{4}\) as \(x \to \pm\infty\text{.}\)

    Example 6.5.14. End Behavior.

    Find the end behavior of \(r(x)=\dfrac{x^3+4x^2-4x-16}{x^2-2x-3}\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    \(r(x) \to x+6\) as \(x \to \pm\infty\text{,}\) .
    Solution.
    Since the degree of the numerator is 3 and the degree of the denominator is 2, the degree of the numerator is one more than the denominator. Therefore, there is an oblique asymptote found using polynomial long division.
    This gives us the oblique asymptote \(y=x+6\text{.}\) Therefore, \(r(x) \to x+6\) as \(x \to \pm\infty\text{.}\)

    Example 6.5.17. End Behavior.

    Find the end behavior of \(r(x)=\dfrac{x^4+8x^3+12x^2-32x-64}{x^2-2x-3}\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    \(r(x) \to x^2+10x+35\) as \(x \to \pm\infty\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Since the degree of the numerator is 4 and the degree of the denominator is 2, the degree of the numerator is larger than the degree of the denominator by 2 or more. Therefore, there is a nonlinear asymptote found using polynomial long division.
    Therefore, the nonlinear asymptote is \(y=x^2+10x+35\text{.}\) Therefore, \(r(x) \to x^2+10x+35\) as \(x \to \pm\infty\text{.}\)

    Subsection 6.5.3 Crossing Horizontal/Oblique/Nonlinear Asymptotes?

    From our graphs, we notice that the graph of a rational function \(r(x)\) may cross its horizontal, oblique, or nonlinear asymptote. These are important points to plot when sketching the graph of \(r(x)\) since we may need to cross these asymptotes to make sure our end behavior is correct.

    Procedure 6.5.20. How To Determine if a Rational Function Crosses Its Horizontal, Oblique, or Nonlinear Asymptote.

    Let \(r(x)=\dfrac{p(x)}{q(x)}\) be a rational function with a horizontal, oblique, or nonlinear asymptote.
    To find where \(r(x)\) crosses its horizontal, oblique, or nonlinear asymptote,
    1. Set \(r(x)\) equal to the asymptote and solve for \(x\text{.}\)
    2. For each solution, evaluate \(r(x)\) to find the corresponding \(y\)-coordinate.
    3. Plot each point \((x,y)\) on the graph of \(r(x)\text{.}\)

    Example 6.5.21. Crossing an Asymptote.

    Find where \(r(x)=\dfrac{x^2+2x}{x^2+1}\) crosses its horizontal asymptote \(y=1\) .
    Answer.
    The graph of \(r(x)\) crosses its horizontal asymptote at \(\left(\frac{1}{2},1\right)\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    To find where \(r(x)\) crosses its horizontal asymptote, we set \(r(x)=1\) and solve for \(x\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} r(x) \amp=1\\ \\ \dfrac{x^2+2x}{x^2+1} \amp=1\\ \\ x^2+2x \amp=x^2+1\\ \\ 2x \amp=1\\ \\ x \amp=\dfrac{1}{2}\\ \end{align*}
    Since the asymptote is horizontal, the \(y\)-coordinate is always \(1\text{.}\) Therefore, there is only one point where the graph crosses the horizontal asymptote:
    \begin{equation*} \left(\frac{1}{2},1\right) \end{equation*}

    Example 6.5.23. Crossing an Asymptote.

    Find where \(r(x)=\dfrac{x^3-3x^2+4}{x^2-2x+2}\) crosses its oblique asymptote \(y=x-1\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    The graph of \(r(x)\) crosses its oblique asymptote at \(\left(\frac{3}{2},\frac{1}{2}\right)\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    To find where \(r(x)\) crosses its oblique asymptote, we set \(r(x)=x-1\) and solve for \(x\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} r(x) \amp=x-1\\ \\ \dfrac{x^3-3x^2+4}{x^2-2x+2} \amp=x-1\\ \\ x^3-3x^2+4 \amp=(x-1)(x^2-2x+2)\\ \\ x^3-3x^2+4 \amp=x^3-3x^2+4x-2\\ \\ -4x+6 \amp=0\\ \\ -4x \amp=-6\\ \\ x\amp=\dfrac{-6}{-4}\\ \\ x \amp=\dfrac{3}{2}\\ \end{align*}
    Evaluating \(r\left(\frac{3}{2}\right)\)
    \begin{align*} r\left(\dfrac{3}{2}\right) \amp=\dfrac{\left(\dfrac{3}{2}\right)^3-3\left(\dfrac{3}{2}\right)^2+4}{\left(\dfrac{3}{2}\right)^2-2\left(\dfrac{3}{2}\right)+2}\\ \\ \amp=\dfrac{\dfrac{27}{8}-\dfrac{27}{4}+4 }{\dfrac{9}{4}-3+2}\\ \\ \amp=\dfrac{5/8}{5/4} \\\\ \amp=\dfrac{5}{8} \cdot \dfrac{4}{5} \\\\ \amp=\dfrac{1}{2} \end{align*}
    Therefore, there is only one point where the graph crosses the oblique asymptote:
    \begin{equation*} \left(\dfrac{3}{2},\frac{1}{2}\right) \end{equation*}
