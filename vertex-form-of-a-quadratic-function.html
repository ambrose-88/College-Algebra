    Section 4.14 The Vertex Form of a Quadratic Function

    Watermark text: DRAFT

    Subsection 4.14.1 Writing Quadratic Equations in Vertex Form

    Definition 4.14.1. The Standard Form of a Quadratic.

    The standard form of a quadratic function is
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    The standard form of a quadratic function is not very useful since the majority of its features, like its vertex and axis of symmetry, are not baked into the algebraic expression \(ax^2 + bx + c\text{.}\) So, while the standard form is useful for solving equations in one variable, it is not as useful when set equal to \(y\) instead of 0. Using the theory of transformations from Chapter 7, mathematicians discovered that the vertex form of the function is the correct way to encapsulate all the important information of the graph.
    🔗

    Theorem 4.14.2. The Vertex Form and Related Information.

    Let \(f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c\) be a quadratic function in standard form. The vertex form of a quadratic function is given by
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = a(x - h)^2 + k, \end{equation*}
    where \(h\) and \(k\) are the coordinates for the vertex of the function.
    🔗
    There are two important definitions related to the vertex form.
    • The vertex of the parabola is the absolute minimum (when \(a > 0\)) or the absolute maximum (when \(a < 0\)) of the function. Note that the vertex is both a local and absolute extremum.
      🔗
    • The axis of symmetry of a parabola is the vertical line that divides the parabola into two mirror images. Every point on one side of this line has a corresponding point on the other side that is the same distance from the line.
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    There are two methods for finding the vertex form from the standard form. The first one uses completing the square to accomplish the transformation.
    🔗

    Example 4.14.4. Using Completing the Square to Convert from Standard to Vertex Form.

    Convert \(f(x) = 3x^2 - 12x + 13\) to vertex form.
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(f(x)= 3(x - 2)^2 + 1\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} f(x)\amp= 3x^2 - 12x + 13 \\\\ f(x) - 13\amp= 3x^2 - 12x \\\\ \frac{f(x)}{3} - \frac{13}{3}\amp= \frac{3x^2}{3} - \frac{12}{3}x \\\\ \frac{f(x)}{3} - \frac{13}{3}\amp= x^2 - 4x \\\\ \frac{f(x)}{3} - \frac{13}{3}\amp= x^2 - 4x \amp \frac{-4}{2}\amp= -2 \amp (-2)^2\amp= 4 \\\\ \frac{f(x)}{3} - \frac{13}{3} + 4\amp= x^2 - 4x + 4 \\\\ \frac{f(x)}{3} - \frac{13}{3} + 4\amp= (x - 2)^2 \\\\ 3\left(\frac{f(x)}{3}\right) - 3\left(\frac{13}{3}\right) + 3(4)\amp= 3(x - 2)^2 \\\\ f(x) - 13 + 12\amp= 3(x - 2)^2 \\\\ f(x) - 1\amp= 3(x - 2)^2 \\\\ f(x)\amp= 3(x - 2)^2 + 1 \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    While completing the square gets the job done, it is very cumbersome and time-consuming to do. It would be nice to have a formula for the \(h\) and \(k\) in the vertex formula. If we complete the square on \(f(x)=ax^2+bx+c\text{,}\) we get
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=a\left(x+\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2+c-\frac{b^2}{4a} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    This tells us that
    🔗
    \begin{align*} h\amp=-\frac{b}{2a} \amp k\amp=c-\frac{b^2}{4a} \end{align*}
    🔗
    But, notice what happens when we evaluate \(f\left(-\frac{b}{2a}\right)\)
    🔗
    \begin{align*} f\left(-\frac{b}{2a}\right)\amp=a\left(-\frac{b}{2a}+\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2+c-\frac{b^2}{4a} \\\\ \amp=a(0)^2+k \\\\ \amp=k \end{align*}
    🔗
    Therefore, the pair of formula you should use and memorize is
    🔗
    \begin{align*} h\amp=-\frac{b}{2a} \amp k\amp=f\left(-\frac{b}{2a}\right) \end{align*}
    🔗

    Theorem 4.14.5. Formula for the Vertex of a Parabola.

    Let \(f(x)=ax^2+bx+c\) be a quadratic function. Then the formula for the vertex is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (h,k)=\left(-\frac{b}{2a},f\left(-\frac{b}{2a}\right)\right) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    We can also rewrite this as the pair of equations
    \begin{align*} h\amp=-\frac{b}{2a}\amp k\amp=f\left(\frac{-b}{2a}\right) \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    While these formulas still look unwieldy in this form, in practice they are very easy to use.
    🔗

    Example 4.14.6. Using the Formula for \(h\) and \(k\) to find the Vertex Form of a Quadratic Function.

    Write \(f(x)=2x^2-4x-6\) in vertex form.
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(f(x)=2(x-1)^2-8\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    Since \(a=2\) and \(b=-4\text{,}\) we have that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} h=-\frac{b}{2a}=-\frac{-4}{2(2)}=1. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Then, evaluate \(f(1)\) to find the value of \(k\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{align*} f(x)\amp=2x^2-4x-6 \\\\ f(1)\amp=2(1)^2-4(1)-6 \\\\ f(1)\amp=2-4-6 \\\\ f(1)\amp=-8 \end{align*}
    🔗
    Therefore, the vertex form is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=2(x-1)^2-8. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 4.14.2 Graphing Quadratic Functions Using the Vertex Form

    The vertex form gives us enough information to easily graph any quadratic function.
    🔗

    Procedure 4.14.7. Graphing Quadratic Functions.

    Let \(f(x) = a(x - h)^2 + k\) be a quadratic function in vertex form. Perform the following steps to plot its graph.
    🔗
    1. Plot the vertex \((h, k)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. To find another point, add \(1\) to \(h\) and add \(a \cdot 1^2\) to \(k\text{.}\) Then plot the point.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. To find an additional point, add \(2\) to \(h\) and add \(a \cdot 2^2\) to \(k\text{.}\) Then plot the point.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Use the axis of symmetry to find and plot the last two points.
      🔗
      🔗
    5. Draw a parabola through all five points.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.14.8. How to Graph a Quadratic Function, \(a > 0\).

    Graph the function \(f(x) = 2x^2 - 4x + 5\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    🔗
    Solution.
    First, we note that \(a = 2\) and \(b = -4\text{.}\) Using the formula for \(h\text{,}\) we get:
    🔗
    \begin{align*} h \amp= -\frac{b}{2a} \\\\ \amp= -\frac{-4}{2(2)} \\\\ \amp= \frac{4}{4} \\\\ \amp= 1 \end{align*}
    🔗
    Next, we compute the value of \(k\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{align*} k \amp= f(1) \\\\ \amp= 2(1)^2 - 4(1) + 5 \\\\ \amp= 2 - 4 + 5 \\\\ \amp= 3 \end{align*}
    🔗
    Therefore, we know the vertex is \((1, 3)\) and the axis of symmetry is the vertical line \(x = 1\text{.}\) We start the graph by plotting the vertex and axis of symmetry.
    🔗
    Next, we compute two additional points. The first point is:
    🔗
    \begin{align*} \text{first additional point } \amp= (h + 1, k + a(1)^2) \\\\ \amp= (1 + 1, 3 + 2(1)^2) \\\\ \amp= (2, 5) \end{align*}
    🔗
    and the second point is:
    🔗
    \begin{align*} \text{second additional point } \amp= (h + 2, k + a(2)^2) \\\\ \amp= (1 + 2, 3 + 2(2)^2) \\\\ \amp= (3, 11) \end{align*}
    🔗
    We plot these two points as well.
    🔗
    Next, we find the two mirrored points. They will have the same \(y\)-coordinates as \((2, 5)\) and \((3, 11)\text{,}\) but the \(x\)-coordinates will be \(h - 1\) and \(h - 2\text{,}\) respectively. Therefore, the mirrored points are \((0,5)\) and \((-1, 11)\text{.}\) We plot these additional points.
    🔗
    Finally, we connect all the points with a parabola to get the final graph.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.14.14. How to Graph a Quadratic Function, \(a < 0\).

    Graph the function \(f(x) = -3x^2 - 12x - 8\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    🔗
    Solution.
    First, identify the coefficients \(a = -3\) and \(b = -12\text{.}\) Using the formula for \(h\text{,}\) we get:
    🔗
    \begin{align*} h \amp= -\frac{b}{2a} \\\\ \amp= -\frac{-12}{2(-3)} \\\\ \amp= \frac{12}{-6} \\\\ \amp= -2 \end{align*}
    🔗
    Next, compute \(k\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{align*} k \amp= f(-2) \\\\ \amp= -3(-2)^2 - 12(-2) - 8 \\\\ \amp= -12 + 24 - 8 \\\\ \amp= 4 \end{align*}
    🔗
    The vertex is \((-2, 4)\) and the axis of symmetry is the vertical line \(x = -2\text{.}\) Begin the graph by plotting the vertex and axis of symmetry.
    🔗
    Now compute two additional points one and two units to the right of the vertex:
    🔗
    \begin{align*} (h + 1, k + a(1)^2) \amp= (-2 + 1, 4 + (-3)(1)^2) \\\\ \amp= (-1, 1) \end{align*}
    🔗
    \begin{align*} (h + 2, k + a(2)^2) \amp= (-2 + 2, 4 + (-3)(2)^2) \\\\ \amp= (0, -8) \end{align*}
    🔗
    Plot these two points.
    🔗
    Reflect across the axis \(x = -2\) to obtain the mirrored points:
    🔗
    \begin{align*} (h - 1, 1) \amp= (-3, 1) \\\\ (h - 2, -8) \amp= (-4, -8) \end{align*}
    🔗
    Plot these mirrored points as well.
    🔗
    Finally, connect the points with a smooth downward-opening parabola to obtain the final graph.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 4.14.3 Graphing Quadratic Functions Using Intercepts

    While the previous method always works, it requires you to remember some unusual formulas. When the parabola crosses the \(x\)-axis, you can instead use the vertex, the \(y\)-intercept, and the \(x\)-intercepts to plot key points. The next example shows how to do this.
    🔗

    Example 4.14.20. How to Graph a Quadratic Function Using Intercepts.

    Graph the function \(f(x) = -2x^2 - 8x - 6.\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Since \(a = -2\) and \(b = -8\text{,}\) we use the formulas for \(h\) and \(k\) to find the vertex.
    🔗
    \begin{align*} h \amp= -\frac{b}{2a} \\\\ \amp= -\frac{-8}{2(-2)} \\\\ \amp= -\frac{8}{4} \\\\ h \amp= -2 \end{align*}
    🔗
    \begin{align*} k \amp= f(-2) \\\\ \amp= -2(-2)^2 - 8(-2) - 6 \\\\ \amp= -8 + 16 - 6 \\\\ k \amp= 2 \end{align*}
    🔗
    Therefore, the vertex is \((-2, 2)\text{.}\) To find the \(x\)-intercepts, we use the quadratic formula:
    🔗
    \begin{align*} x \amp= \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \\\\ \amp= \frac{-(-8) \pm \sqrt{(-8)^2 - 4(-2)(-6)}}{2(-2)} \\\\ \amp= \frac{8 \pm \sqrt{64 - 48}}{-4} \\\\ \amp= \frac{8 \pm \sqrt{16}}{-4} \end{align*}
    🔗
    \begin{align*} x \amp= \frac{8 - 4}{-4} \amp x \amp= \frac{8 + 4}{-4} \\\\ x \amp= \frac{4}{-4} \amp x \amp= \frac{12}{-4} \\\\ x \amp= -1 \amp x \amp= -3 \end{align*}
    🔗
    Therefore, the \(x\)-intercepts are \((-1, 0)\) and \((-3, 0)\text{.}\) To find the \(y\)-intercept, we evaluate \(f(0)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(0) = -2(0)^2 - 8(0) - 6 = -6 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Therefore, the \(y\)-intercept is \((0, -6)\text{.}\) We then plot all four points and sketch the parabola through them to obtain the graph.
    🔗
