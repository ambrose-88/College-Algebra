    Section 5.3 Zeros and Multiplicity

    Subsection 5.3.1 The Complete Linear Factorization of \(p(x)\)

    The ultimate goal of this chapter is to find the complete linear factorization of a polynomial function \(p(x)\text{.}\)

    Theorem 5.3.1.

    The complete linear factorization of a degree \(n\) polynomial function \(p(x)\) with leading coefficient \(a_n\) is given by the formula
    \begin{equation*} p(x)=a_n(x-c_1)(x-c_2)\cdots(x-c_{n-1})(x-c_n), \end{equation*}
    where \(c_1\text{,}\) \(c_2\text{,}\) \(\ldots\text{,}\) \(c_{n-1}\text{,}\) \(c_n\) are the \(n\) (not necessarily distinct) solutions to the equation \(p(x)=0\text{.}\)
    Lets think about what this statement says.
    1. There is a relationship between factoring and solutions to polynomial equations.
    2. A degree \(n\) polynomial function has \(n\) solutions.
    3. Since the \(c_i\) can be the same value, a solution can be repeated.
    4. Since this statement is a theorem, there is a proof that this complete linear factorization can always be found.
    While the proof is beyond the scope of this course, our main focus is on the first implication: there is a relationship between factoring and solving equations. We already this relationship in Chapter 4: the Zero Product Theorem. We also said in Chapter 4 that the Zero Product Theorem works no matter how many factors there are. That means if
    \begin{equation*} a_n(x-c_1)(x-c_2)\cdots(x-c_n)=0, \end{equation*}
    then finding the solution to the equation \(p(x)=0\) is equivalent to solving the \(n\) equations
    \begin{equation*} x-c_1=0\qquad x-c_2=0\qquad\cdots\qquad x-c_n=0. \end{equation*}
    This is very abstract. So, lets look at a concrete example of this idea in action.

    Example 5.3.2. Using the Complete Linear Factorization to Solve an Equation.

    The complete linear factorization of the polynomial function
    \begin{equation*} p(x)=-2x^4-4x^3+26x^2+28x-48 \end{equation*}
    is
    \begin{equation*} p(x)=-2(x-3)(x-1)(x+3)(x+4). \end{equation*}
    Use the complete linear factorization of the function to solve the equation \(p(x)=0\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    • \(\displaystyle x=-4\)
    • \(\displaystyle x=-3\)
    • \(\displaystyle x=1\)
    • \(\displaystyle x=3\)
    Solution.
    We will replace the original definition of \(p(x)\) with its complete linear factorization and then use the Zero Product Property to solve the equation.
    \begin{align*} p(x)\amp=0 \\\\ -2x^4-4x^3+26x^2+28x-48\amp=0 \\\\ -2(x-3)(x-1)(x+3)(x+4)\amp=0 \\\\ (x-3)(x-1)(x+3)(x+4)\amp=0 \\ \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} x-3\amp=0 \amp x-1\amp=0 \amp x+3\amp=0 \amp x+4\amp=0 \\\\ x\amp=3 \amp x\amp=1 \amp x\amp=-3 \amp x\amp=-4 \end{align*}
    It is mysterious, though, how we went from
    \begin{equation*} p(x)=-2x^4-4x^3+26x^2+28x-48 \end{equation*}
    to
    \begin{equation*} p(x)=-2(x-3)(x-1)(x+3)(x+4). \end{equation*}
    None of our factoring techniques show us how to factor this polynomial. Starting in Section5.7, we will develop techniques that will allow us to factor these types of polynomials. But, for the moment, we will explore the relationship between the solutions of \(p(x)=0\) and the \(x\)-intercepts of the graph of \(p(x)\text{.}\)

    Subsection 5.3.2 The Zeroes of a Polynomial Function

    Definition 5.3.3.

    A complex number \(c\) is a zero of a polynomial function if \(p(c)=0\text{.}\)

    Note 5.3.4.

    Zeros are also called the roots or solutions of the polynomial equation \(p(x)=0\text{.}\)
    Wait a complex number \(c\text{?}\) You might ask yourself if that is a typo, but that is correct. Some of the \(c_i\) in our complete linear factorization will be complex! Lets look at two examples and see how this plays out.

    Example 5.3.5. Finding the zeroes of a polynomial function with distinct, real zeroes.

    Find the zeroes of the polynomial \(p(x)=6x^3+9x^2-30x-45.\)
    Answer.
    • \(\displaystyle x=-\frac{3}{2}\)
    • \(\displaystyle x=\sqrt{5}\)
    • \(\displaystyle x=-\sqrt{5}\)
    Solution.
    Find the zeroes of \(p(x)=6x^3+9x^2-30x-45\) is another way to say solve the equation \(6x^3+9x^2-30x-45=0.\) Therefore, we get the following calculation.
    \begin{align*} p(x)\amp=0 \\\\ 6x^3+9x^2-30x-45\amp=0 \\\\ 2x^3+3x^2-10x-15\amp=0 \\\\ (2x^3-10x)+(3x^2-15)\amp=0 \\\\ 2x(x^2-5)+3(x^2-5)\amp=0 \\\\ (x^2-5)(2x+3)\amp=0 \\\\ (x-\sqrt{5})(x+\sqrt{5})(2x+3)\amp=0 \\\\ x-\sqrt{5}\amp=0 \amp x+\sqrt{5}\amp=0 \amp 2x+3\amp=0 \\\\ x\amp=\sqrt{5} \amp x\amp=-\sqrt{5} \amp x\amp=-\frac{3}{2} \end{align*}
    Now, you might note that \(2x+3=0\) is not of the form \(x-c=0\text{,}\) which is required for the complete linear factorization. There is a theorem that helps us with that.

    Theorem 5.3.6. Rewriting Linear Factors that Have Rational Solutions.

    Any linear factor of the form \((ax-b)\) can always be rewritten as \(\left(x-\frac{b}{a}\right)\text{.}\)
    In other words, we could have rewritten \((2x+3)\) as \(\left(x+\frac{3}{2}\right)\text{,}\) but that requires an extra step that is really not necessary when we are solving for \(x\text{.}\)
    Now, lets solve an equation with complex solutions.

    Example 5.3.7. Finding the zeroes of a polynomial function with one real solution and two complex solutions.

    Find the zeroes of the function \(p(x)=x^3+27.\)
    Answer.
    • \(\displaystyle x=-3\)
    • \(\displaystyle x=\frac{3}{2}-\frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2}i\)
    • \(\displaystyle x=\frac{3}{2}+\frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2}i\)
    Solution.
    Since this is a sum of cubes formula, we will first apply that formula.
    \begin{align*} p(x)\amp=0 \\\\ x^3+27\amp=0 \\\\ (x+3)(x^2-3x+9)\amp=0 \end{align*}
    Notice that the factor \(x^2-3x+9\) is irreducible since its discriminant is negative. But if we allow complex solutions, we can use the quadratic formula to find the complex solutions without having to factor \(x^2-3x+9\) over the complex numbers. The calculation for the quadratic formula is given below.
    \begin{align*} x\amp=\frac{-b\pm\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} \\\\ x\amp=\frac{-(-3)\pm\sqrt{(-3)^2-4(1)(9)}}{2(1)} \\\\ x\amp=\frac{3\pm\sqrt{9-36}}{2} \\\\ x\amp=\frac{3\pm\sqrt{-27}}{2} \\\\ x\amp=\frac{3\pm3i\sqrt{3}}{2} \\\\ x\amp=\frac{3}{2}\pm\frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2}i \end{align*}
    Now, we can finish solving the equation.
    \begin{align*} (x+3)(x^2-3x+9)\amp=0 \\\\ x+3\amp=0\amp x^2-3x+9\amp=0\\\\ x\amp=-3\amp x\amp=\frac{3}{2}\pm\frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2}i\\ \end{align*}

    Note 5.3.8.

    We did not write out the complete linear factorization when solving Example5.3.7 since this tends to confuse students at first. For the record, the complete linear factorization is
    \begin{equation*} (x+3)\left(x-\left(\frac{3}{2}-\frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2}i\right)\right)\left(x-\left(\frac{3}{2}+\frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2}i\right)\right)=0. \end{equation*}
    Also, notice that we have see products like
    \begin{equation*} \left(x-\left(\frac{3}{2}-\frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2}i\right)\right)\left(x-\left(\frac{3}{2}+\frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2}i\right)\right) \end{equation*}
    before in Example4.8.7.

    Subsection 5.3.3 The Multiplicity of a Zero

    When we stated Theorem5.3.1, we mentioned that the solutions to the polynomial equation \(p(x)=0\) can be repeated. This leads to the idea of multiplicity, which has two major uses in this chapter:
    1. Visualizing how the graph of a polynomial crosses or bounces off the \(x\)-axis.
    2. As a trick to simplify solving polynomial inequalities.
    We first start by defining multiplicity.

    Definition 5.3.9. The Multiplicity of a Zero.

    Let \(p(x)\) be any polynomial function that has a factor of \((x-c)^m\text{,}\) where \(m\geq 1\text{.}\)
    • The exponent \(m\) is called the multiplicity of the zero \(x=c\text{.}\)
    • If \(m>1\text{,}\) then \(x=c\) is called a repeated zero of \(p(x)\text{.}\)
    Lets illustrate this definition with an example.

    Example 5.3.10. Finding the Multiplicities of a Factored Polynomial.

    Let \(p(x)=x^3(x-4)^2(x+1)\text{.}\) Find the zeroes and their multiplicities.
    Answer.
    • \(x=0\) has multiplicity \(3\)
    • \(x=4\) has multiplicity \(2\)
    • \(x=-1\) has multiplicity \(1\)
    Solution.
    To find the zeroes, we will set \(p(x)=0\) and use the Zero Product Property.
    \begin{align*} p(x)\amp=0 \\\\ x^3(x-4)^2(x+1)\amp=0 \\\\ x^3\amp=0 \amp (x-4)^2\amp=0 \amp x+1\amp=0 \\\\ \sqrt[3]{x^3}\amp=\sqrt[3]{0} \amp \sqrt{(x-4)^2}\amp=0 \amp x\amp=-1 \\\\ x\amp=0 \amp x-4\amp=0 \amp x\amp=-1 \\\\ x\amp=0 \amp x\amp=4 \amp x\amp=-1 \end{align*}
    To find the multiplicity of the zeroes, we just have to look back at which factor generated each solution.
    • Since \(x=0\) came from the factor \(x^3\) and \(x^3=(x-0)^3\text{,}\) the multiplicity of \(x=0\) is \(3\text{.}\)
    • Since \(x=4\) came from the factor \((x-4)^2\text{,}\) the multiplicity of \(x=4\) is \(2\text{.}\)
    • Since \(x=-1\) came from the factor \(x+1\) and \(x+1=(x+1)^1\text{,}\) the multiplicity of \(x=-1\) is \(1\text{.}\)
    Note that both \(x=0\) and \(x=4\) are repeated zeroes since their multiplicities are greater than 1.

    Subsection 5.3.4 Visualizing Zeroes as \(x\)-intercepts

    The real zeroes of a polynomial correspond to the \(x\)-intercepts of the graph. On top of that, the multiplicity of the zero also dictates the shape of the graph near the \(x\)-intercept. The key feature is not the exact value of the multiplicityit is whether the multiplicity is \(1\text{,}\) an odd number greater than \(1\text{,}\) or an even number.

    Theorem 5.3.11. The Shape of the Graph Near an \(x\)-intercept.

    Suppose \(x=c\) is a zero of a polynomial \(p\) that has multiplicity \(m\text{.}\) The following figures illustrate the different ways a polynomial can behave near the \(x\)-intercept \((c,0)\text{.}\)

    Example 5.3.15. Determine if the Graph Will Cross or Bounce at an \(x\)-intercept.

    Let \(p(x)=-2(x-3)^2(x+4)\text{.}\) Find the zeroes, their multiplicities, and classify whether the graph will cross or bounce at the zeroes.
    Solution.
    • There is a zero at \(x=3\) with even multiplicity. The graph will bounce off the \(x\)-axis at \((3,0)\text{.}\)
    • There is a zero at \(x=-4\) with odd multiplicity. The graph will cross the \(x\)-axis at \((-4,0)\text{.}\)
