    Section 6.3 Zeroes of the Denominator

    Subsection 6.3.1 Domains of Rational Functions

    Theorem 6.3.1. The Domain of a Rational Function.

    The domain of a rational function \(r(x)\) are all values of \(x\) except those that make the denominator equal to zero before canceling any common factors.

    Example 6.3.2. Finding the Domain.

    Determine the domain of the function \(r(x)=\dfrac{x^2-4}{x^2-1}\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    \((-\infty, -1) \cup (-1, 1) \cup (1, \infty)\)
    Solution.
    To find the domain, we need to identify the values of \(x\) that make the denominator equal to zero. To do this, we factor the denominator as follows:
    \begin{gather*} r(x)=\dfrac{x^2-4}{x^2-1}\\\\ =\dfrac{x^2-4}{(x-1)(x+1)} \end{gather*}
    By the Factor Theorem, the linear factors of the denominator correspond to values of \(x\) that make the denominator equal to zero. Thus, the domain is all real numbers except \(x=1\) and \(x=-1\text{.}\)
    In interval notation, the domain is \((-\infty, -1) \cup (-1, 1) \cup (1, \infty)\text{.}\)

    Example 6.3.3. Finding the Domain.

    Determine the domain of the function \(r(x)=\dfrac{x^2-4}{x^2-5x+6}\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    \((-\infty, 2) \cup (2, 3) \cup (3, \infty)\)
    Solution.
    To find the domain, we need to identify the values of \(x\) that make the denominator equal to zero. To do this, we factor the denominator as follows:
    \begin{gather*} r(x)=\dfrac{x^2-4}{x^2-5x+6}\\\\ =\dfrac{(x-2)(x+2)}{(x-2)(x-3)} \end{gather*}
    While it is tempting to cancel off the common factor of \(x-2\text{,}\) this will obscure the fact that \(x=2\) makes the denominator equal to zero in the original equation. We must not perform the cancellation until we determine the domain
    By the Factor Theorem, the linear factors of the denominator correspond to values of \(x\) that make the denominator equal to zero. Thus, the domain is all real numbers except \(x=2\) and \(x=3\text{.}\)
    In interval notation, the domain is \((-\infty, 2) \cup (2, 3) \cup (3, \infty)\text{.}\)

    Note 6.3.4. Cancellations and Domain.

    If we wanted to graph the previous example, we will need to perform the cancellation at some point to continue the graphing process. How do keep track of the domain in this case without explicitly writing down the entire domain in interval notation?
    When we perform the cancellation, we can write the condition that \(x \neq 2\) next to the simplified version of the function to remind ourselves that \(x=2\) is not in the domain. The following calculation shows us how to do this:
    \begin{gather*} r(x)=\dfrac{x^2-4}{x^2-5x+6}\\\\ =\dfrac{(x-2)(x+2)}{(x-2)(x-3)}\\\\ =\dfrac{x+2}{x-3}, \quad x \neq 2 \end{gather*}
    This leads us to an important idea for graphing rational functions: the graph of a rational function is the same as its simplified version but with holes or asymptotes whenever we cancel off linear factors. Well explore this idea in more detail shortly.

    Subsection 6.3.2 Arrow Notation

    Subsection 6.3.3 Vertical Asymptotes

    Definition 6.3.7. Vertical Asymptote.

    The vertical line \(x=a\) is a vertical asymptote of any fully simplified, rational function \(r(x)\) if \(r(x) \to \pm \infty\) as \(x \to a^{\pm}\text{.}\)

    Note 6.3.8. Note.

    A function can never cross a vertical asymptote.

    Procedure 6.3.10. How to Find a Vertical Asymptote of a Rational Function.

    Let \(r(x)\) be any rational function. To find the vertical asymptotes of the graph, follow this procedure.
    1. Completely factor the numerator and denominator into their linear and irreducible quadratic factors.
    2. Find all the linear factors of the denominator. By the Factor Theorem, each linear factor \(x-c\) corresponds to a zero of the denominator and will make \(r(x)\) undefined.
    3. For each linear factor of the denominator, determine if it also appears in the numerator.
      • If it doesnt, then \(x=c\) is a vertical asymptote of \(r(x)\text{.}\)
      • If the linear factor also appears in the numerator, then we have a vertical asymptote at \(x=c\) only if the multiplicity of the denominator is larger that the multiplicity of the numerator.

    Example 6.3.11. Finding the Vertical Asymptotes of a Rational Function.

    Find all the vertical asymptotes for the rational function \(r(x)=\frac{1}{x^3+3x^2-4}\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    The following vertical lines are vertical asymptotes.
    • \(\displaystyle x=-2\)
    • \(\displaystyle x=1\)
    Solution.
    The first step is to completely factor the numerator and denominator. Since the numerator is just a constant, we only have to factor the denominator.
    To factor the denominator, first note that the coefficients of \(r(x)\) sum to 1; therefore, the Test for \(\pm 1\) tells us that \(x=1\) is a zero the denominator. We can use synthetic division to factor the polynomial.
    Notice that the quotient is a perfect square trinomial and can be factored as \((x+2)^2\text{.}\) Therefore, \(x^3+3x^2-4=(x-1)(x+2)^2\text{,}\) and our fully factored rational function is
    \begin{equation*} r(x)=\frac{1}{(x-1)(x+2)^2} \end{equation*}
    By the Factor Theorem, the linear factors of the denominator correspond to values of \(x\) that make the denominator equal to zero. Since neither linear factor appears in the numerator, we can conclude that the vertical lines \(x=-2\) and \(x=1\) are vertical asymptotes of the graph.

    Example 6.3.14. Finding the Vertical Asymptotes of a Rational Function.

    Find all the vertical asymptotes for the rational function \(r(x)=\frac{x^3+3x^2-4}{x^2-2x+1}\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    The following vertical lines are vertical asymptotes.
    • \(\displaystyle x=1\)
    Solution.
    In the last example, we saw that \(x^3+3x^2-4=(x-1)(x+2)^2\text{.}\) Notice that the denominator is a perfect square trinomial; therefore, it factors as \(x^2-2x+1=(x-1)^2\text{.}\) Therefore, our completely factored polynomial is
    \begin{equation*} r(x)=\frac{(x-1)(x+2)^2}{(x-1)^2} \end{equation*}
    Since the multiplicity of \(x-1\) is 1 in the numerator and 2 in the denominator, the denominator has the larger multiplicity. Therefore, the vertical line \(x=1\) is an asymptote.

    Example 6.3.16. Finding the Vertical Asymptotes of a Rational Function.

    Find all the vertical asymptotes for the rational function \(r(x)=\frac{(x-1)^2(x+2)^2}{(x-1)^2}\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    The following vertical lines are vertical asymptotes.
    • \(\displaystyle x=1\)
    Solution.
    This rational function is already factored for us. Notice that the factor \(x-1\) appears in both the numerator and denominator. Unlike the previous example, the multiplicities of \(x-1\) are the same in the numerator and denominator. Since the denominator does not have a larger multiplicity, there is not a vertical asymptote at \(x=1\text{.}\) In fact, since there are no other linear factors in the denominator to generate an asymptote, this rational function does not have any vertical asymptote.

    Note 6.3.20. Simplifying and Finding Multiplicities For the Denominator.

    When finding the multiplicities for the zeroes of the denominator, be sure to use the fully simplified version of the rational function. Any common factors that were canceled off will affect the multiplicities of the zeroes of the denominator.

    Example 6.3.21. Finding the Zeroes of the Denominator and Their Multiplicities.

    Find the zeroes of the denominator of the rational function \(r(x)=\frac{x^2-9}{(x-1)(x+2)^2}\) and state their multiplicities.
    Answer.
    • \(x=1\) with multiplicity 1
    • \(x=-2\) with multiplicity 2
    Solution.
    The denominator is already factored for us. By the Factor Theorem, the linear factors of the denominator correspond to values of \(x\) that make the denominator equal to zero.
    Since the linear factors are \(x-1\) and \(x+2\text{,}\) the zeroes of the denominator are \(x=1\) and \(x=-2\text{.}\)
    Since the factor \(x-1\) has multiplicity 1 and the factor \(x+2\) has multiplicity 2, then the zero \(x=1\) has multiplicity 1 and the zero \(x=-2\) has multiplicity 2.
