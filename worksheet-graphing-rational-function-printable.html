    

    Definition A.6.1. Definition.

    A function \(r(x)\) is rational if there exist two polynomial functions \(p(x)\) and \(q(x)\) such that
    \begin{equation*} r(x) = \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} \end{equation*}

    1. Determine if a Function is Rational or Not.

    Determine if the following functions are rational or not.
    1. \(\displaystyle f(x) = \frac{3x - 4}{x^2 - x + 3}\)
    2. \(\displaystyle f(x) = \frac{3}{x^2 - 1}\)
    3. \(\displaystyle f(x) = x^2 + x + 1\)
    4. \(\displaystyle f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 2\sqrt{x} + 1}{x^4 + 1}\)

    Definition A.6.2. Zeroes of a Rational Function.

    Let \(r(x)=\frac{p(x)}{d(x)}\) be any fully simplified rational function. Then \(x=c\) is a zero of \(r(x)\) only if it is in the domain of \(r(x)\) and a zero of \(p(x)\text{.}\)

    Definition A.6.3. The Multiplicities of Zeroes for Rational Functions.

    Let \(r(x)=\frac{p(x)}{d(x)}\) be any fully simplified rational function. The zero \(x=c\) has multiplicity \(m\) only if \((x-c)^m\) is a factor of \(p(x)\text{.}\)

    2. Finding the Multiplicities of Zeroes of a Fully Simplified Rational Function.

    Find the zeroes and their multiplicities for the rational function \(r(x)=\dfrac{x^3-x^2-8x+12}{x^2-3x-4}\text{.}\)

    Theorem A.6.4. The Domain of a Rational Function.

    The domain of a rational function \(r(x)\) are all values of \(x\) except those that make the denominator equal to zero before canceling any common factors.

    3. Finding the Domain.

    Determine the domain of the function \(r(x)=\dfrac{x^2-4}{x^2-5x+6}\text{.}\)

    Definition A.6.7. Vertical Asymptote.

    The vertical line \(x=a\) is a vertical asymptote of any fully simplified, rational function \(r(x)\) if \(r(x) \to \pm \infty\) as \(x \to a^{\pm}\text{.}\)

    Note A.6.8. Note.

    A function can never cross a vertical asymptote.

    Procedure A.6.10. How to Find a Vertical Asymptote of a Rational Function.

    Let \(r(x)\) be any rational function. To find the vertical asymptotes of the graph, follow this procedure.
    1. Completely factor the numerator and denominator into their linear and irreducible quadratic factors.
    2. Find all the linear factors of the denominator. By the Factor Theorem, each linear factor \(x-c\) corresponds to a zero of the denominator and will make \(r(x)\) undefined.
    3. For each linear factor of the denominator, determine if it also appears in the numerator.
      • If it doesnt, then \(x=c\) is a vertical asymptote of \(r(x)\text{.}\)
      • If the linear factor also appears in the numerator, then we have a vertical asymptote at \(x=c\) only if the multiplicity of the denominator is larger than the multiplicity of the numerator.

    4. Finding the Vertical Asymptotes of a Rational Function.

    Find all the vertical asymptotes for the rational function \(r(x)=\frac{1}{x^3+3x^2-4}\text{.}\)

    5. Finding the Vertical Asymptotes of a Rational Function.

    Find all the vertical asymptotes for the rational function \(r(x)=\frac{(x-1)^2(x+2)^2}{(x-1)^2}\text{.}\)
    While the multiplicity of a linear factor in the numerator tells us how we cross or bounce off the x-axis, the multiplicity of a linear factor in the denominator tells us how the graph behaves near a vertical asymptote. In fact, if we think of infinity as a point that we cannot see on the coordinate plane, then the multiplicity of a linear factor in the denominator tells us how the graph crosses or bounces off of infinity.

    Note A.6.13. Simplifying and Finding Multiplicities For the Denominator.

    When finding the multiplicities for the zeroes of the denominator, be sure to use the fully simplified version of the rational function. Any common factors that were canceled off will affect the multiplicities of the zeroes of the denominator.

    6. Finding the Zeroes of the Denominator and Their Multiplicities.

    Find the zeroes of the denominator of the rational function \(r(x)=\frac{x^2-9}{(x-1)(x+2)^2}\) and state their multiplicities.

    Theorem A.6.14. Theorem.

    Let \(r(x)=\dfrac{p(x)\cdot (x-c)^n}{q(x)\cdot (x-c)^m}\) be any rational function whose numerator and denominator have a common factor of \((x-c)\text{,}\) where the integer \(m\geq 1\) and the integer \(n\geq 0\text{.}\)
    Then \(r(x)\) is undefined at \(x=c\) since we cannot divide by zero, but comparing the multiplicities of \(x=c\) in the numerator and denominator can still yield information about the behavior of the graph near \(x=c\text{.}\) There are three different behaviors that can occur, as summarized in the following table.

    7. Hole vs. Asymptote at \(x=2\).

    Determine if the function \(r(x)=\dfrac{x}{(x-1)(x-2)}\) has a hole or a vertical asymptote at \(x=2\text{.}\)

    8. Hole vs. Asymptote.

    Determine if the function \(r(x)=\dfrac{x(x-2)}{(x-1)(x-2)}\) has a hole or a vertical asymptote at \(x=2\text{.}\)

    9. Hole vs. Asymptote.

    Determine if the function \(r(x)=\dfrac{x(x-2)^3}{(x-1)(x-2)}\) has a hole or a vertical asymptote at \(x=2\text{.}\)

    Definition A.6.16. Horizontal Asymptote.

    The horizontal line \(y=b\) is a horizontal asymptote for \(f(x)\) if \(y \to b\) as \(x \to \pm\infty\text{.}\)

    Definition A.6.18. Oblique (Slant) Asymptote.

    The line \(y=mx+b\) is an oblique asymptote for \(f(x)\) if \(y\) approaches the graph of \(y=mx+b\) as \(x \to \pm\infty\text{.}\)

    Definition A.6.20. Nonlinear Asymptote.

    A nonlinear function \(g(x)\) is a nonlinear asymptote for \(f(x)\) if \(y\) approaches the graph of \(g(x)\) as \(x \to \pm\infty\text{.}\)

    Theorem A.6.22. End Behavior Criterion.

    Let \(r(x)=\dfrac{p(x)}{q(x)}\) be a rational function. Let \(n\) be the degree of \(p(x)\) with leading coefficient \(a_n\text{,}\) and let \(m\) be the degree of \(q(x)\) with leading coefficient \(b_m\text{.}\) The end behavior is determined by comparing \(n\) and \(m\) as summarized below.

    10. End Behavior.

    Find the end behavior of \(r(x)=\dfrac{4x^3-9x^2+4x-6}{5x^{8}-17x^7+3x-4}\text{.}\)

    11. End Behavior.

    Find the end behavior of \(r(x)=\dfrac{9x^{12}+3x^5-2x^2}{12x^{12}-4x^9+3x^7+x}\text{.}\)

    12. End Behavior.

    Find the end behavior of \(r(x)=\dfrac{x^3+4x^2-4x-16}{x^2-2x-3}\text{.}\)

    13. End Behavior.

    Find the end behavior of \(r(x)=\dfrac{x^4+8x^3+12x^2-32x-64}{x^2-2x-3}\text{.}\)

    Procedure A.6.24. How To Determine if a Rational Function Crosses Its Horizontal, Oblique, or Nonlinear Asymptote.

    Let \(r(x)=\dfrac{p(x)}{q(x)}\) be a rational function with a horizontal, oblique, or nonlinear asymptote.
    To find where \(r(x)\) crosses its horizontal, oblique, or nonlinear asymptote,
    1. Set \(r(x)\) equal to the asymptote and solve for \(x\text{.}\)
    2. For each solution, evaluate \(r(x)\) to find the corresponding \(y\)-coordinate.
    3. Plot each point \((x,y)\) on the graph of \(r(x)\text{.}\)

    14. Crossing an Asymptote.

    Find where \(r(x)=\dfrac{x^3-3x^2+4}{x^2-2x+2}\) crosses its oblique asymptote \(y=x-1\text{.}\)

    Procedure A.6.25. Procedure.

    Let \(r(x)=\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}\) be any rational function. Use the following steps to graph the function.
    1. Find the \(y\)-intercept (if it exists) and express it as a point \((0, r(0))\text{.}\)
    2. Factor the numerator and denominator, but do not cancel any factors yet.
    3. Find the zeroes of the denominator (if they exist) and determine the type of behavior (vertical asymptote or hole) for each zero.
    4. Cancel off the common factors.
    5. Determine the end behavior of the function.
    6. Determine if the graph crosses the horizontal, oblique, or nonlinear asymptote. Plot any points of intersection.
    7. Find the zeroes of the numerator (if they exist) and their multiplicities.
      • Express your \(x\)-intercepts as \((x,0)\) and graph them as solid dots unless they correspond to a hole, in which case use an open dot.
      • Use the multiplicities to determine whether the graph crosses or bounces at each intercept.
    8. Determine the multiplicities of the remaining zeroes of the denominator. The multiplicities of the denominator determine the graphs behavior near the vertical asymptotes.
      • Even multiplicities mean that the graph bounces off infinity.
      • Odd multiplicities mean that the graph crosses from positive to negative infinity, or vice versa.
    9. Use the intercepts, end behavior, and multiplicities to draw a general sketch of the graph. Plot additional points as needed to further define the shape of the graph.

    Graph the function \(r(x)=\dfrac{x-2}{(x+1)(x-2)(x-3)^2}\text{.}\)
