    Section 5.11 The Rational Root Theorem

    Theorem 5.11.1. Rational Root Theorem.

    Let \(p(x)\) be any polynomial function with leading coefficient \(a_n\) and constant term \(a_0\text{.}\) Then the possible rational roots are given by
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\text{all possible factors of } a_0}{\text{all possible factors of } a_n} \end{equation*}

    Example 5.11.2. Finding the Possible Rational Zeroes of a Polynomial Function, Version 1.

    Find all the possible rational zeroes of \(p(x) = 2x^5 - 4x^3 - x^2 + 23x + 3\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    \(\pm 1, \pm 3, \pm\frac{1}{2}, \pm\frac{3}{2}\)
    Solution.
    The factors of \(3\) are \(\pm 1\) and \(\pm 3\text{,}\) and the factors of \(2\) are \(\pm 1\) and \(\pm 2\text{.}\) Therefore, the possible rational zeroes are
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\pm 1, \pm 3}{\pm 1, \pm 2} \end{equation*}
    When we simplify this list, we have
    \begin{equation*} \pm 1, \pm 3, \pm\frac{1}{2}, \pm\frac{3}{2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Example 5.11.3. Finding the Possible Rational Zeroes of a Polynomial Function, Version 2.

    Find all the possible rational zeroes of \(p(x) = 6x^5 - 7x^2 - x + 8\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    \(\pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 4, \pm 8, \pm\frac{2}{3}, \pm\frac{1}{3}, \pm\frac{4}{3}, \pm\frac{8}{3}\)
    Solution.
    The factors of \(8\) are \(\pm 1\text{,}\) \(\pm 2\text{,}\) \(\pm 4\text{,}\) and \(\pm 8\text{,}\) and the factors of \(6\) are \(\pm 1\text{,}\) \(\pm 2\text{,}\) \(\pm 3\text{,}\) \(\pm 6\text{.}\) Therefore, the possible rational zeroes are
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 4, \pm 8}{\pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 3, \pm 6} \end{equation*}
    When we simplify this list, we have
    \begin{equation*} \pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 4, \pm 8, \pm\frac{2}{3}, \pm\frac{1}{3}, \pm\frac{4}{3}, \pm\frac{8}{3}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Example 5.11.4. Finding Zeroes of a Polynomial Function.

    Find all the zeroes of \(p(x) = 9x^3 + 21x^2 - 17x + 3\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    • \(\displaystyle x = -3\)
    • \(\displaystyle x = \frac{1}{3}\)
    Solution.
    The factors of \(3\) are \(\pm 1\text{,}\) \(\pm 3\text{,}\) and the factors of \(9\) are \(\pm 1\text{,}\) \(\pm 3\text{,}\) \(\pm 9\text{.}\) Therefore, the possible rational zeroes are
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\pm 1, \pm 3}{\pm 1, \pm 3, \pm 9} \end{equation*}
    When we simplify this list, we have
    \begin{equation*} \pm 1, \pm 3, \pm\frac{1}{3}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Now, there are two ways to continue. We could do a bunch of synthetic divisions until we found a remainder of zero. Or, we can use technology to start evaluating the potential zeroes until we find one. In this case, I used Desmos to define the function and built a table until I found that \(x = -3\) is a zero of \(p(x)\text{.}\)
    Since \(x = -3\) is a zero of \(p(x)\text{,}\) we can use synthetic division to factor \(p(x)\text{.}\)
    We also observe that \(q(x) = 9x^2 - 6x + 1\) is a perfect square trinomial and factors as \(q(x) = (3x - 1)^2\text{.}\) Therefore, our final factorization is
    \begin{equation*} p(x) = (3x - 1)^2(x + 3) \end{equation*}
    Therefore, our zeroes are \(x = -3\) and \(x = \frac{1}{3}\text{.}\)

    Example 5.11.7. Finding Zeroes of a Polynomial Function, Version 2.

    Find all the zeroes of \(p(x) = 72x^4-138x^3+41x^2+8x-3\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    • \(\displaystyle x = -\frac{1}{4}\)
    • \(\displaystyle x = \frac{1}{3}\)
    • \(\displaystyle x = \frac{3}{2}\)
    Solution.
    The factors of \(-3\) are \(\pm 1\text{,}\) \(\pm 3\text{,}\) and the factors of \(72\) are \(\pm 1\text{,}\) \(\pm 2\text{,}\) \(\pm 3\text{,}\) \(\pm 4\text{,}\) \(\pm 6\text{,}\) \(\pm 8\text{,}\) \(\pm 9\text{,}\) \(\pm 12\text{,}\) \(\pm 18\text{,}\) \(\pm 24\text{,}\) \(\pm 36\text{,}\) \(\pm 72\text{.}\) Therefore, the possible rational roots are
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\pm 1, \pm 3}{\pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 3, \pm 4, \pm 6, \pm 8, \pm 9, \pm 12, \pm 18, \pm 24, \pm 36, \pm 72} \end{equation*}
    When we simplify this list, we have
    \begin{equation*} \pm 1, \pm 3, \pm\frac{1}{2}, \pm\frac{3}{2}, \pm\frac{1}{3}, \pm\frac{1}{4}, \pm\frac{3}{4}, \pm\frac{1}{6}, \pm\frac{1}{8}, \pm\frac{3}{8}, \pm\frac{1}{9}, \pm\frac{1}{12}, \pm\frac{1}{18}, \pm\frac{1}{24}, \pm\frac{1}{36}, \pm\frac{1}{72}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Now, there are two ways to continue. We could do a bunch of synthetic divisions until we found a remainder of zero. Or, we can use technology to start evaluating the potential zeroes until we find one. In this case, I used Desmos to define the function and built a table until I found that \(x = \frac{3}{2}\) is a zero of \(p(x)\text{.}\)
    Since \(x = \frac{3}{2}\) is a zero of \(p(x)\text{,}\) we can use synthetic division to factor \(p(x)\text{.}\)
    Therefore, we have the factorization
    \begin{equation*} p(x)=(72x^3-30x^2-4x+2)\left(x-\frac{1}{3}\right)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Now, we find the possible rational roots of the quotient. The factors of \(2\) are \(\pm 1\) and \(\pm 2\text{,}\) and the factors of \(72\) are \(\pm 1\text{,}\) \(\pm 2\text{,}\) \(\pm 3\text{,}\) \(\pm 4\text{,}\) \(\pm 6\text{,}\) \(\pm 8\text{,}\) \(\pm 9\text{,}\) \(\pm 12\text{,}\) \(\pm 18\text{,}\) \(\pm 24\text{,}\) \(\pm 36\text{,}\) \(\pm 72\text{.}\) Therefore, the possible rational zeroes are
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\pm 1, \pm 2}{\pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 3, \pm 4, \pm 6, \pm 8, \pm 9, \pm 12, \pm 18, \pm 24, \pm 36, \pm 72} \end{equation*}
    When we simplify this list, we have
    \begin{equation*} \pm 1, \pm 2, \pm\frac{1}{2}, \pm\frac{1}{3}, \pm\frac{2}{3}, \pm\frac{1}{4}, \pm\frac{1}{6}, \pm\frac{1}{8}, \pm\frac{2}{9}, \pm\frac{1}{9}, \pm\frac{1}{12}, \pm\frac{1}{18}, \pm\frac{1}{24}, \pm\frac{1}{36}, \pm\frac{1}{72}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Now, there are two ways to continue. We could do a bunch of synthetic divisions until we found a remainder of zero. Or, we can use technology to start evaluating the potential zeroes until we find one. In this case, I used Desmos to define the quotient and built a table until I found that \(x = \frac{1}{3}\) is a zero of \(q(x)\text{.}\)
    Since \(x = \frac{3}{2}\) is a zero of \(p(x)\text{,}\) we can use synthetic division to factor \(p(x)\text{.}\)
    Therefore, we have the factorization
    \begin{align*} p(x)\amp = 72x^4-138x^3+41x^2+8x-3\\\\ p(x)\amp=(72x^3-30x^2-4x+2)\left(x-\frac{1}{3}\right)\\\\ p(x)\amp=(72x^2-6x-6)\left(x-\frac{1}{3}\right)\left(x-\dfrac{3}{2}\right)\\\\ p(x)\amp=6(12x^2-x-1)\left(x-\frac{1}{3}\right)\left(x-\dfrac{3}{2}\right)\\\\ p(x)\amp=6(12x^2-4x+3x-1)\left(x-\frac{1}{3}\right)\left(x-\dfrac{3}{2}\right)\\\\ p(x)\amp=6[4x(3x-1)+1(3x-1)]\left(x-\frac{1}{3}\right)\left(x-\dfrac{3}{2}\right)\\\\ p(x)\amp=6(3x-1)(4x+1)\left(x-\frac{1}{3}\right)\left(x-\dfrac{3}{2}\right)\\\\ p(x)\amp=(3x-1)(4x+1)(3x-1)(2x-3)\\\\ p(x)\amp=(3x-1)^2(4x+1)(2x-3)\\ \end{align*}
    By the Factor Theorem, the zeroes are \(x=\frac{3}{2}\text{,}\) \(x=\frac{1}{3}\text{,}\) and \(x=-\frac{1}{4}\)

    Example 5.11.12. Graphing a Degree 4 Polynomial.

    Graph the function \(p(x) = x^4-11x^3+42x^2-64x+32\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    Solution.
    The first step is to determine the end behavior of \(p(x)\text{.}\) Since the leading term \(x^4\) has a positive coefficient and even degree, both ends of the graph look similar to \(y=x^4\text{.}\) Therefore, the graph travels up and to the right as \(x\) approaches \(+\infty\text{.}\) Similarly, the graph travels up and to the left as \(x\) approaches \(-\infty\text{.}\)
    Next, since the constant term is \(a_0 = 32\text{,}\) the \(y\)-intercept is \((0,32)\text{.}\)
    Now, we have to find the zeroes of the polynomial. You should always try the Test for \(\pm 1\) first. Adding the coefficients of \(p(x)\text{,}\) we get the following calculation.
    \begin{equation*} 1-11+42-62+32=0 \end{equation*}
    Since the coefficients sum to 0, then \(x=1\) is a zero of \(p(x)\text{.}\) We use synthetic division to factor the polynomial.
    Now, we can factor the polynomial as follows:
    \begin{equation*} p(x)=(x^3-10x^2+32x-32)(x-1) \end{equation*}
    Now, we start over with the quotient \(q(x)=x^3-10x^2+32x-32\text{.}\) The Test for \(\pm 1\) fails for \(q(x)\text{;}\) so, we have to resort to the Rational Roots Theorem. The factors of \(-32\) are \(\pm 1\text{,}\) \(\pm 2\text{,}\) \(\pm 4\text{,}\) \(\pm 8\text{,}\) \(\pm 16\text{,}\) \(\pm 32\text{,}\) and the factors of the leading coefficient \(1\) are \(\pm 1\text{.}\) Therefore, the possible rational zeroes are
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 4, \pm 8, \pm 16, \pm 32}{\pm 1} \end{equation*}
    When we simplify this list, we have
    \begin{equation*} \pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 4, \pm 8, \pm 16, \pm 32\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since we already know that \(\pm 1\) are not zeroes, we start building our table with \(\pm 2\text{.}\) The last row in our table shows that \(x=2\) is a zero of \(q(x)\)
    Since \(x = 2\) is a zero of \(q(x)\text{,}\) we can use synthetic division to factor \(q(x)\text{.}\)
    Notice that \(x^2-8x+16\) is a perfect square trinomial and factors as \((x-4)^2\text{.}\) Therefore, our complete factorization is given by the following calculation.
    \begin{align*} p(x)\amp=x^4-11x^3+42x^2-64x+32\\\\ p(x)\amp=(x^3-10x^2+32x-32)(x-1)\\\\ p(x)\amp=(x^2-8x+16)(x-2)(x-1)\\\\ p(x)\amp=(x-4)^2)(x-2)(x-1)\\ \end{align*}
    Therefore, the \(x\)-intercepts are \((4,0)\text{,}\) \((2,0)\text{,}\) and \((1,0)\text{.}\) The multiplicities are \(2\text{,}\) \(1\text{,}\) and \(1\) respectively. We add these points to the graph.
