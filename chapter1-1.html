    Section 1.1 Graphing Points on the Coordinate Plane

    Graphing is one of the fundamental tools in Algebra. As visual creatures, humans can see solutions as pictures instead of only letters and numbers on a page. This helps people notice patterns in data that might be difficult to find when it is expressed only as text. Over the course of this semester, there will be a strong focus on understanding and interpreting graphs from both a mathematical and real-world point of view.
    🔗

    Subsection 1.1.1 Ordered Pairs

    Points on a number line are described by a single number. We use notation such as \(x=3\) to show a value on the number line; we graph the point by placing a solid dot three spaces to the right of a place labeled 0 on the number line.
    🔗
    When we move to a coordinate plane, we need two numbers to describe a point. One number describes the horizontal (left/right) movement, and the other describes the vertical (up/down) movement. In theoretical mathematics, the variable \(x\) represents horizontal movement, and the variable \(y\) represents vertical movement. The following definition shows how we represent points on a coordinate plane.
    🔗

    Notation 1.1.2.

    Let \(x\) and \(y\) be any real numbers. An ordered pair is a list of numbers, written as \((x, y)\text{,}\) where the order in which you write the numbers matters (e.g. \((3,4) \ne (4,3)\)).
    🔗
    🔗
    There are additional definitions related to ordered pairs.
    🔗

    Definition 1.1.3.

    Let \((x, y)\) be an ordered pair.
    • \(x\) is known as the x-coordinate (or informally as the first coordinate).
      🔗
    • \(y\) is known as the y-coordinate (or informally as the second coordinate).
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    While coordinates are often written as ordered pairs, they can also be written as a pair of equations. The next two examples show us how to convert from ordered pairs to a pair of equations, and vice versa.
    🔗

    Example 1.1.4. Convert Coordinates to Ordered Pairs.

    What point corresponds to the pair of equations \(x = -2\) and \(y = \frac{3}{2}\text{?}\)
    🔗
    Express the point as an ordered pair.
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(\left(-2, \frac{3}{2}\right)\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    Since every point on the coordinate plane can be expressed as an ordered pair \((x, y)\text{,}\) this pair of equations can be represented by the ordered pair \(\left(-2, \frac{3}{2}\right)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.1.5. Convert Ordered Pair to Coordinates.

    Let the ordered pair \((2.54, -4.12)\) represent a point on the coordinate plane.
    🔗
    What are the values for \(x\) and \(y\text{?}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    \begin{align*} x \amp =2.54 \\ y \amp =-4.12 \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    Since every point on the coordinate plane can be expressed as an ordered pair \((x, y)\text{,}\) then:
    🔗
    - \(x = 2.54\) since \(2.54\) is the first coordinate.
    🔗
    - \(y = -4.12\) since \(-4.12\) is the second coordinate.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 1.1.2 Visualizing The Coordinate Plane

    Subsubsection 1.1.2.1 Coordinate Axes

    Now that we know how to represent points on a coordinate plane in symbols, we need to know how to visualize the coordinate plane. The coordinate plane uses two number lines called coordinate axes to describe the locations of all the ordered pairs on the coordinate plane. The following procedure describes how to correctly draw the coordinate axes and label them.
    🔗
    Procedure 1.1.6. Drawing a Coordinate Plane.
    1. We start by drawing a number line and label it as \(x\text{.}\) We call this number line the \(x\)-axis.
      🔗
    2. Next, we draw another number line that intersects the first line at a right angle and label it as \(y\text{.}\) We call this second number line the \(y\)-axis.
      🔗
    3. Finally, we label each axis with a scale. The scale does not necessarily have to be the same on each axis.
      🔗
    4. The scales determine the coordinate grid, a network of horizontal and vertical lines that helps you determine where points belong on the coordinate plane. Drawing the coordinate grid is optional, but often helpful.
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    The following figure shows a coordinate plane with the \(x\)- and \(y\)-axes labeled and a coordinate grid drawn in.
    🔗
    Note 1.1.8. Tip.
    When learning how to graph on the coordinate plane, use graph paper to help set up your scale and coordinate grid.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsubsection 1.1.2.2 The Origin and Rectangular Coordinates

    One of the most important points on the coordinate plane is the origin, which is represented by the ordered pair \((0,0)\text{.}\) On many coordinate planes, the origin is where the coordinate axes intersect. But in many real-world problems, the coordinate axes might be visible. We draw a pair of axes that act as stand-ins for the \(x\)- and \(y\)-axes, and theses stand-ins will intersect at a point other than the origin. This can help emphasize patterns in data that would be hidden forced the axes and the origin to always appear in our graph.
    🔗
    The coordinate plane is often called the rectangular coordinate plane because the origin and any other point form opposite corners of a rectangle. For example, the point \((3,4)\) refers to rectangle width \(3\) on the \(x\)-axis and length \(4\) on the \(y\)-axis. But, the point \((4,3)\) refers to a rectangle with length \(4\) on the \(x\)-axis and width \(3\) on the \(y\)-axis. This is why the pairs are ordered. Switching the order of \(x\) and \(y\) defines a different point on the plane since it defines a different rectangle.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsubsection 1.1.2.3 The Quadrants of a Coordinate Plane

    When the coordinate axes cross at the origin, they create four subregions called quadrants. While quadrants play a minimal role in a course like College Algebra, they are very important in some of the future courses you will take.
    🔗
    Definition 1.1.11. Quadrants.
    • Quadrant 1 contains all the points to the right of the \(y\)-axis (\(x>0\)) and above the \(x\)-axis (\(y>0\)).
      🔗
    • Quadrant 2 contains all the points to the left of the \(y\)-axis (\(x<0\)) and above the \(x\)-axis (\(y>0\)).
      🔗
    • Quadrant 3 contains all the points to the left of the \(y\)-axis (\(x<0\)) and below the \(x\)-axis (\(y<0\)).
      🔗
    • Quadrant 4 contains all the points to the right of the \(y\)-axis (\(x>0\)) and below the \(x\)-axis (\(y<0\)).
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Note 1.1.13.
    Note: If a point lies on either the \(x\)- or \(y\)-axis, then it is an intercept and is not part of any quadrant. We will learn more about intercepts in Section 2.10.
    🔗
    🔗
    Example 1.1.14. Determine In Which Quadrant a Point Lies.
    Consider the following coordinate plane with a standard scale and the points labeled \(A\) through \(H\text{.}\) Determine which quadrant each point lies in.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Points \(A\) and \(H\) are in Quadrant 1.
    🔗
    Points \(B\) and \(E\) are in Quadrant 2.
    🔗
    Points \(D\) and \(G\) are in Quadrant 3.
    🔗
    Points \(C\) and \(F\) are in Quadrant 4.
    🔗
