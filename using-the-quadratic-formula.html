    Subsection 4.11.1 Solving Equations Using the Quadratic Formula

    Example 4.11.1. Solving an Equation with Two Real Roots.

    Solve the equation \(4x^2 + 13x + 10 = 0\) using the Quadratic Formula.
    🔗
    Answer.
    • \(\displaystyle x = -2\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(\displaystyle x = -\frac{5}{4}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    The equation \(4x^2 + 13x + 10 = 0\) can be solved by substituting \(a = 4\text{,}\) \(b = 13\text{,}\) and \(c = 10\) into the Quadratic Formula \(x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{align*} x \amp= \frac{-13 \pm \sqrt{13^2 - 4(4)(10)}}{2(4)} \\\\ \amp= \frac{-13 \pm \sqrt{169 - 160}}{8} \\\\ \amp= \frac{-13 \pm \sqrt{9}}{8} \\\\ \amp= \frac{-13 \pm 3}{8} \\\\ x \amp= \frac{-13 + 3}{8} \amp x\amp= \frac{-13 - 3}{8} \\\\ x \amp= \frac{-10}{8} \amp x\amp= \frac{-16}{8} \\\\ x \amp= -\frac{5}{4}\amp x\amp= -2 \\ \end{align*}
    🔗
    Thus, the solutions are \(x = -2\) and \(x = -\frac{5}{4}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.11.2. Solving an Equation with One Real Root.

    Solve the equation \(16x^2 + 24x + 9 = 0\) using the Quadratic Formula.
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(x = -\frac{3}{4}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    Substitute \(a = 16\text{,}\) \(b = 24\text{,}\) and \(c = 9\) into \(x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{align*} x \amp= \frac{-24 \pm \sqrt{24^2 - 4(16)(9)}}{2(16)} \\\\ \amp= \frac{-24 \pm \sqrt{576 - 576}}{32} \\\\ \amp= \frac{-24 \pm \sqrt{0}}{32} \\\\ \amp= \frac{-24 \pm 0}{32} \\\\ x \amp= \frac{-24}{32} \\\\ \amp= -\frac{3}{4} \\ \end{align*}
    🔗
    Thus, the (repeated) solution is \(x = -\frac{3}{4}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.11.3. Solving an Equation with Two Complex Solutions.

    Solve the equation \(x^2 - 4x + 13 = 0\) using the Quadratic Formula.
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(x = 2 \pm 3i\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    Substitute \(a = 1\text{,}\) \(b = -4\text{,}\) and \(c = 13\) into \(x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{align*} x \amp= \frac{-(-4) \pm \sqrt{(-4)^2 - 4(1)(13)}}{2(1)} \\\\ \amp= \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{16 - 52}}{2} \\\\ \amp= \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{-36}}{2} \\\\ \amp= \frac{4 \pm 6i}{2} \\\\ \amp= \frac{4}{2}\pm\frac{6}{2}i \\\\ x \amp= 2 \pm 3i \\\\ x \amp= 2 + 3i \amp x\amp= 2 - 3i \\ \end{align*}
    🔗
    Thus, the solutions are \(x = 2 + 3i\) and \(x = 2 - 3i\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 4.11.2 The Discriminant

    The number and types of solutions for Example 4.11.1, Example 4.11.2, and Example 4.11.3 can be summarized as follows.
    • The value of \(b^2-4ac\) for Example 4.11.1 is positive. This gave us two, real-valued solutions.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The value of \(b^2-4ac\) for Example 4.11.2 equals zero. This gave us a unique, real-valued solution.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The value of \(b^2-4ac\) for Example 4.11.3 is negative. This gave us a pair of complex numbers that are conjugates of one another.
      🔗
      🔗
    The value of \(b^2-4ac\) is called the discriminant since it discriminates between the different types of solutions a quadratic equation can have.
    🔗

    Definition 4.11.4. The Discriminant.

    We call the value \(b^2 - 4ac\) the discriminant of the quadratic equation. We denote the discriminant by the letter \(D\text{,}\) which gives us the equation
    \begin{equation*} D = b^2 - 4ac\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    In particular, the discriminant tells us the following information:
    🔗
    • If \(D > 0\text{,}\) then the quadratic equation has two distinct, real-valued solutions.
      🔗
      🔗
    • If \(D = 0\text{,}\) then the quadratic equation has a unique, real-valued solution.
      🔗
      🔗
    • If \(D < 0\text{,}\) then the quadratic equation has a pair of complex-valued solutions that are complex conjugates of one another.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.11.5. Use the Discriminant to Determine the Number and Types of Solutions, Version 1.

    Determine the number and type of solutions for the equation \(6x^2 + x - 40 = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    Two distinct, real-valued solutions.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    In this case, we have \(a = 6\text{,}\) \(b = 1\text{,}\) and \(c = -40\text{.}\) We calculate the discriminant as follows.
    🔗
    \begin{align*} D \amp= b^2 - 4ac \\\\ \amp= (1)^2 - 4(6)(-40) \\\\ \amp= 1 + 960 \\\\ \amp= 961 \\ \end{align*}
    🔗
    Since \(D > 0\text{,}\) we have two distinct, real-valued solutions.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.11.6. Use the Discriminant to Determine the Number and Types of Solutions, Version 2.

    Determine the number and type of solutions for the equation \(16x^2 - 8x + 1 = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    One unique, real-valued solution.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    In this case, we have \(a = 16\text{,}\) \(b = -8\text{,}\) and \(c = 1\text{.}\) We calculate the discriminant as follows.
    🔗
    \begin{align*} D \amp= b^2 - 4ac \\\\ \amp= (-8)^2 - 4(16)(1) \\\\ \amp= 64 - 64 \\\\ \amp= 0 \\ \end{align*}
    🔗
    Since \(D = 0\text{,}\) we have a unique, real-valued solution.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.11.7. Use the Discriminant to Determine the Number and Types of Solutions, Version 3.

    Determine the number and type of solutions for the equation \(x^2 - 10x + 74 = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    Two complex-valued solutions that are complex conjugates of one another.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    In this case, we have \(a = 1\text{,}\) \(b = -10\text{,}\) and \(c = 74\text{.}\) We calculate the discriminant as follows.
    🔗
    \begin{align*} D \amp= b^2 - 4ac \\\\ \amp= (-10)^2 - 4(1)(74) \\\\ \amp= 100 - 296 \\\\ \amp= -196 \\ \end{align*}
    🔗
    Since \(D < 0\text{,}\) the equation has two complex-valued solutions that are complex conjugates of one another.
    🔗
