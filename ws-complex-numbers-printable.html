    Worksheet A.4.2 Complex Numbers
    

    Definition A.4.15. Definition of \(i\).

    The imaginary number \(i\) is the positive solution to the equation
    \begin{equation*} x^2+1=0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Powers of \(i\) are really easy to calculate, and unlike real numbers, there are only four values you have to memorize since the powers cycle through four unique values.
    🔗
    \begin{align*} i^0 \amp= 1 \amp i^4 \amp= (i^2)^2 = (-1)^2 = 1\\ i^1 \amp= i \amp i^5 \amp= i^4 \cdot i^1 = 1 \cdot i = i\\ i^2 \amp= -1 \amp i^6 \amp= i^4 \cdot i^2 = (1)(-1) = -1\\ i^3 \amp= i^2 \cdot i^1 = -i \amp i^7 \amp= i^4 \cdot i^3 = -i \end{align*}
    🔗
    The pattern continues forever like this. If we look at the first four exponents—\(0,1,2,3\)—these are the possible remainders you get when you divide an integer by \(4\text{.}\) Notice that the powers of \(i\) that have the same remainder after dividing the exponent by \(4\) will have the same value.
    🔗

    Theorem A.4.16. Divisibility Rule for 4.

    An integer is divisible by \(4\) if its last two digits form a number that is divisible by \(4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Note A.4.17. Divisibility Rule in Terms of Remainders.

    In the language above, this means that a number is divisible by \(4\) if you divide the last two digits by \(4\) and get a remainder of \(0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Procedure A.4.18. Computing Powers of \(i\).

    To evaluate \(i^n\) for any positive integer \(n\text{,}\) complete the following steps.
    🔗
    1. Divide the last two digits of \(n\) by \(4\) using long division and determine the remainder. You should get \(0\text{,}\) \(1\text{,}\) \(2\text{,}\) or \(3\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The remainder tells you which power of \(i\) to use from this list.
      \begin{align*} i^0 \amp= 1 \amp i^1 \amp= i \amp i^2 \amp= -1 \amp i^3 \amp= -i \end{align*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    1. Compute a Power of \(i\).

    Compute \(i^{487562438573735}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition A.4.19. Complex Number.

    Let \(a\) and \(b\) be any real numbers. A complex number is any number of the form
    \begin{equation*} a+bi\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Do complex numbers really exist? It turns out that complex numbers are just another way to describe points \((a,b)\) in the coordinate plane. For example, the point \((3,4)\) can also be represented by the complex number \(3+4i\text{.}\) But there is a subtle difference. Instead of thinking of it as the corner of a rectangle, we think of \(3+4i\) as an arrow pointing from the origin to the point \((3,4)\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Procedure A.4.21. How to Add Complex Numbers.

    Let \(a+bi\) and \(c+di\) be any two complex numbers. Then
    \begin{equation*} (a+bi)+(c+di)=(a+c)+(b+d)i\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    In other words, just add like terms.
    🔗
    🔗

    2. Adding Two Complex Numbers.

    Perform the operation \((3-4i)+(-2+9i)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Procedure A.4.22. How to Subtract Complex Numbers.

    Let \(a+bi\) and \(c+di\) be any two complex numbers. Then
    \begin{equation*} (a+bi)-(c+di)=(a-c)+(b-d)i\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    In other words, distribute the negative and add like terms.
    🔗
    🔗

    3. Subtracting Two Complex Numbers.

    Perform the operation \((-3+2i)-(-5+3i)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Procedure A.4.23. How to Multiply Complex Numbers.

    Let \(a+bi\) and \(c+di\) be any two complex numbers. Then
    \begin{equation*} (a+bi)(c+di)=(ac-bd)+(ad+bc)i\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    In other words, just FOIL it out and simplify using \(i^2=-1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    4. Multiply Two Complex Numbers.

    Perform the operation \((-6+5i)(2-i)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition A.4.24. The Complex Conjugate.

    Let \(a+bi\) be any complex number. Then its complex conjugate is the complex number
    \begin{equation*} a-bi\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    5. Find the Complex Conjugate of a Number.

    Determine the complex conjugate of the number \(3-5i\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    6. Find the Complex Conjugate of a Purely Imaginary Number.

    Determine the complex conjugate of the number \(2i\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    7. Find the Complex Conjugate of a Real Number.

    Determine the complex conjugate of the number \(7\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem A.4.25. Sum of Squares Formula.

    For any real numbers \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) it is always true that
    \begin{equation*} a^2+b^2=(a+bi)(a-bi)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    In other words, the sum of squares is the product of a complex number and its complex conjugate.
    🔗
    🔗

    8. Solve an Equation by Factoring.

    by factoring.

    🔗

    9. Computing FOIL with Complex Numbers and Variables.

    Perform the operation
    \begin{equation*} (x-(2+3i))(x-(2-3i))\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Theorem A.4.26. Pulling out the Negative as \(i\).

    Let \(r > 0\text{.}\) Then \(\sqrt{-r} = i\sqrt{r}\text{.}\)
    🔗
